5 Pointz
1993 ‚Äì 2014
45-46 Davis St, Long Island City, Queens, NYC

"The Institute of Higher Burnin'" ‚Äî the world's premier graffiti mecca. A 200,000 sq ft former Neptune Meter factory, covered floor-to-ceiling in aerosol art, visible from the 7 train. For over a decade, curator Jonathan Cohen (Meres One) transformed it into sacred ground ‚Äî named for the five boroughs of NYC coming together as one. Then one night, it was all erased.

  • 12 factory buildings on 2.9 acres, curated by Meres One from 2002
  • 11,000+ murals painted over its final decade by artists from around the world
  • Whitewashed overnight on Nov 19, 2013 by owner Jerry Wolkoff ‚Äî no warning given
  • Demolished 2014, replaced by luxury condo towers (1,115 units)
  • Artists won $6.75M in VARA lawsuit ‚Äî landmark ruling that graffiti is protected art
  • Banksy's last message of his NYC residency: "Save 5pointz. Bye."

"What if 5 Pointz couldn't be whitewashed?" ‚Äî that's Drop City. A landlord destroyed decades of culture overnight. AR graffiti can't be demolished. Can't be bulldozed for luxury condos. Can't be erased by a coat of white paint at 3 AM. The 5 Pointz story is Drop City's origin wound ‚Äî the proof that physical walls will always be vulnerable, and that digital walls are forever.

Graffiti Research Lab
2005 ‚Äì 2011
Eyebeam OpenLab, Brooklyn, NYC

Founded by Evan Roth and James Powderly at Eyebeam, GRL pioneered the fusion of technology and graffiti ‚Äî proving that code could be a spray can, that a laser pointer could tag a skyscraper, and that the best tools for public expression should be free and open-source.

  • L.A.S.E.R. Tag ‚Äî laser projection graffiti on buildings, live and enormous
  • LED Throwies ‚Äî magnetic LED lights, went viral with 2M+ views, sparking a movement
  • GML (Graffiti Markup Language) ‚Äî open format for sharing graffiti motion data across tools
  • EyeWriter ‚Äî eye-tracking graffiti for ALS patients, now in MoMA's permanent collection
  • Open-sourced everything: free tools, HOW TO guides, full source code
  • Political + playful: laser-projected "Impeach Cheney" on buildings, renamed Verizon signage to "NSA"
‚ö° Connection to Drop City

GRL proved that open-source tools for public expression change everything. Their GML format ‚Äî a universal language for graffiti data ‚Äî is a direct ancestor of Drop City's drawing engine. The EyeWriter showed that accessibility isn't optional, it's core. And their ethos ‚Äî build it, open-source it, give it away ‚Äî is Drop City's operating principle.

Drop City
1965 ‚Äì 1977
Trinidad, Colorado

America's first rural hippie commune. Four art students from the University of Kansas ‚Äî Gene Bernofsky, JoAnn Bernofsky, Richard Kallweit, and Clark Richert ‚Äî bought land near Trinidad, Colorado and built geodesic domes from car roofs and scrap materials. The deed stipulated the land was "forever free and open to all people." The entire commune was a live-in work of art they called "Drop Art."

  • Won Buckminster Fuller's Dymaxion Award in 1967 for innovative geodesic dome construction
  • Built from salvaged car tops, scrap metal, and found materials ‚Äî radical resourcefulness
  • Informed by Allan Kaprow's happenings, John Cage, Robert Rauschenberg, and Buckminster Fuller
  • "Drop Art" ‚Äî continuing a concept from their university days, where art was something you dropped into the world
  • Abandoned by 1977, but spawned dozens of intentional communities across America
  • Deed stipulated land was "forever free and open to all people"
‚ö° Connection to Drop City

The namesake. Public space as commons. Radical openness. Art as a way of life. Geodesic and mathematical beauty. The original Drop City was physical and impermanent ‚Äî founded on an ideal that the world wasn't ready to sustain. The new Drop City is digital and permanent. Same vision: space that belongs to everyone, forever free and open to all people. This time, no one can abandon it because the commons lives in code.

"Forever free and open to all people."

Dead Drops
2010 ‚Äì Present
Started in NYC, now worldwide

Berlin-based artist Aram Bartholl, during his Eyebeam residency in NYC, cemented USB flash drives into walls, buildings, and curbs across the city ‚Äî creating anonymous, offline, peer-to-peer file-sharing nodes in public space. "Un-cloud your files in cement!" Each drive installed empty except for a readme.txt explaining the project. Anyone can participate. Anyone can install a dead drop.

  • First 5 installed Oct 2010: MakerBot (Brooklyn), Empire Fulton Ferry Park (DUMBO), New Museum (Bowery), Union Square, Eyebeam (Chelsea)
  • Featured in MoMA's "Talk to Me" exhibition (2011)
  • Open participation model ‚Äî anyone can install a dead drop and submit its GPS location
  • Each dead drop installed empty with only a readme.txt ‚Äî content emerges from the community
  • Anonymous by design ‚Äî no accounts, no logins, no tracking
  • Now 2,300+ dead drops in 50+ countries worldwide
‚ö° Connection to Drop City

Dead Drops is the closest ancestor to Drop City's core mechanic: location-anchored content sharing in public space. Anonymous participation. The physical-digital bridge. An open participation model where the community creates the content. Dead Drops used USB drives cemented in walls ‚Äî Drop City uses AR anchored to GPS coordinates. Same idea, evolved. The wall is now the air. The USB drive is now augmented reality. The cement is now code.
